Simple RPG

Created by David J. Setser and Dharma Bellamkonda

Introduction

This document describes a system for tabletop role-playing games designed to be adaptable to any setting.

Skills

In this role-playing system, all tasks (including tasks in combat such as attacking and defending) are based on skills. A task is anything a character can try to do. A skill is a quantitative score that represents how well a character can perform tasks that require the use of that skill. Each skill's value is equal to the sum of the skill's associated ability bonus, education bonus and talent bonus. The list of skills available to characters is defined by the setting rules and the game master.

When a character uses a skill to attempt a task, the controlling player (or game master) performs a skill check to determine whether the attempt succeeds or fails. To perform a skill check, a player rolls two six-sided dice and adds their character's skill bonuses, conditional modifiers and trait modifiers to the outcome of the dice roll. The character succeeds if the total skill check is higher than the skill check target's opposed skill check or the skill check's difficulty check. Otherwise, the character fails the task. Note that if a game master determines that a task is completely impossible within the setting, the skill check always fails.

Players always win ties against non-player characters or difficulty checks.

There are two types of skill checks: opposed checks and difficulty checks.

Opposed checks are used when the use of a skill involves competing against or targeting an animate character that is non-compliant or resisting. The target performs their own defensive skill check, using a skill appropriate to the situation. If the offensive character's total skill check is higher than the defensive character's total skill check, the offensive character succeeds. If the defensive character's total skill check is higher than the offensive character's total skill check, the offensive character fails. Opposed skill checks are most commonly used for attacking and defending during combat, but are also applicable to many non-combat situations.

Difficulty checks are used whenever an opposed check is not applicable to the situation. The game master decides on a number that represents how difficult the skill check should be. If the player's skill check is higher than the difficult check, the character succeeds. Otherwise, the character fails the task.

Critical successes and/or critical failures can be optionally implemented by the setting rules or game master. A critical success can be represented by applying a +6 conditional modifier when a player rolls "sixes" (two sixes). A critical failure can be represented by applying a -6 conditional modifier when a player rolls "snake eyes" (two ones).

Ability Bonus

Ability bonuses are six quantitative scores that describe a character's natural abilities in broad areas. An ability bonus of 0 represents an average human, while an ability bonus of 6 represents the highest ability score a human can have without supernatural or technological enhancement. Ability bonuses generally do not change after character creation without significant roleplaying events. Ability bonuses may be negative, indicating below-average ability in that area.

  • Strength: Muscular strength.
  • Dexterity: Agility, reflexes, balance, and fine motor skills.
  • Constitution: Physical health and hardiness.
  • Intelligence: Learning and reasoning skills.
  • Wisdom: Awareness, intuition and common sense.
  • Charisma: Personality and ability to influence others.

    • Each skill is associated with a single ability. The ability bonus distribution for player characters and the associated ability for each skill is defined by the setting rules and the game master.

    Education Bonus

    Education bonuses are quantitative measures of a character's formal training and study in a particular skill. Each skill is associated with an education bonus specific to that skill. Education bonuses range from 0 to 12. Refer to the following scale to compare education bonus values to the United States education system.

    Bonus Skill Level Equivalent Education
    0 Unskilled No Education
    2 Beginner Some School
    4 Novice High School
    6 Apprentice Associate's Degree

    Education bonuses are defined at character creation based on the character's previous training and education. Education bonuses generally do not change after character creation without additional education through roleplaying events. It is up to the individual setting rules, the game master and the players to determine reasonable education bonuses for characters.

    Talent Bonus

    Talent bonuses are quantitative measures of a character's experience and practice at a skill. Talents can be increased by spending talent points, which are the primary means by which characters increase in power. Talent points are rewarded by the game master at game milestones such as completing a quest, advancing the story, or simply at the end of each play session. These are comparable to character levels in other game systems. Players may immediately spend a talent point to increase a single skill's talent bonus by 1.

    Conditional Modifier

    The circumstances of a situation may have a positive or negative effect on your character's skills. These are quantitatively represented by conditional modifiers. Conditional modifiers are defined and applied by the setting rules and the game master. Examples of conditional modifiers include:

  • A character wears set of heavy armor, applying a +2 conditional modifier to their defenses against weapons but also applying a -4 conditional modifier to skills involving physical exertion due to the extra weight.
  • A character fires an arrow from atop a wall at an enemy below, applying a +2 conditional modifier to the attack due to the height difference.
  • A character fails an opposed roll against another character's intimidation, applying a -1 conditional modifier to all skill checks during their next phase due to shaken nerves.
  • A player thinks of an unexpected solution to a problem, granting a +2 conditional modifier to their attempt to execute the solution as a reward for player creativity.

    • Conditional modifiers should be used by the game master to encourage creative roleplaying and problem solving. There should always be some way for players to gain a conditional modifier, to encourage creative and interesting roleplaying.

    Trait Modifier

    Traits represent special or unusual ways a character uses their skills. Traits are defined by the setting rules and the game master and are assigned to characters by on character backgrounds and development. Traits are rarely purely negative, but may include both positive and negative aspects. Examples of traits include:

  • Polar Explorer: You spent time exploring a polar region and are experienced at surviving in cold conditions. Gain a +2 trait modifier to Survival checks against cold conditions.
  • Tinkerer: You love learning how things work. You may dissemble a mechanical object to understand its internal functions. You must pass a Crafting check to correctly reassemble the item.
  • Photosensitive: Your eyes are extra sensitive to light. Gain a +2 trait modifier to Perception checks in low-light and dark environments, but gain a -1 trait modifier in bright environments.
  • Optimism: You try to make the best out of terrible situations. If any non-combat skill check results in a failure, you may choose to repeat the roll. You must accept the new result.

    • Combat

    During combat and other similar situations, an additional set of rules is implemented to manage the condition of the characters and the order and timing of tasks.

    Condition

    A character's condition represents their ability to function and act. The core rules specify two condition types, health and fatigue. Each condition type has a number of condition levels representing different stages of function. Healthy characters start at the normal level of their condition. As a character becomes progressively more injured or tired in a fight, their appropriate condition level is reduced and they incur negative conditional modifiers to all skill checks. The conditional modifiers from multiple condition types stack.

    A character at the lowest level of any condition type cannot defend themselves and are said to be downed. A downed character is completely unable to act or defend themselves against attacks. If a downed character is hit by an attack, they are immediately killed. If a character is downed as a direct result of another character's attack, the attacker may choose to immediately kill the character without consuming an action.

    If a character is at the lowest level of their health condition, they are dying. Dying characters will eventually die permanently. The length of time it takes for character to permanently die should be determined by the game master based on the severity of the character's injuries and the setting rules. Medical care should extend this time.

    If a character is at the lowest level of their fatigue condition, they are incapacitated. Incapacitated characters are unconsious and will eventually wake up (if they are not dying, of course!). The length of time it takes for character to wake up should be determined by the game master based on the severity of the character's fatigue and the setting rules.

    Characters can fully restore their condition levels though full restoration events, such as resting overnight to restore fatigue or receiving appropriate extended rest and medical care to restore health. Characters can also regain a single level of each condition type through temporary means of treating their injuries, such as first aid treatment to restore a single level of health or a short period of rest to restore a single level of fatigue. Once a character has restored a condition by one level, they are unable to restore any additional levels of that condition until they undergo a full restoration event.

    The relative power tier of different characters is represented by the number of levels of their condition types. More powerful characters have more condition levels and are more difficult to defeat.

    Setting player characters at the Heroic tier is recommended, but they may be set at any tier appropriate to the setting and intended difficulty.

    Phase and Action Order

    Combat is divided into phases during which all members of a side each perform actions. Typically, this means that all players perform their actions during the players' phase, then all enemy characters perform their actions during the enemy phase.

    Within a phase, the characters can perform their actions in any order they choose. If the players do not have a preference for action order, they can simply take turns going around the table clockwise.

    The side that acts first is determined by the role playing situation, such as which side takes the other by surprise. If multiple sides start at the same time, the phase order can be decided by rolling one six-sided die for each side and going in order from highest roll to lowest.

    Remember, players always win ties against non-player characters!

    Actions

    An action a task that a character can attempt to execute. Characters can attempt multiple actions during a phase. Actions are resolved with skill checks just as in non-combat situations. The number of actions a character can attempt during a phase is determined by the complexity of the actions. An action's complexity is a quantitative representation of how much time and attention an action requires. Each unit of complexity represents approximately three seconds in which a character can work towards a task. The complexity of a given action is defined by the setting rules and the game master. Normally, a character can attempt up to two complexity's worth of actions per phase. Character can attempt multiple actions per phase, and a character does not have to attempt all their actions at the same time within a phase.

    Some simple actions have a complexity of zero. However, this does not mean a player has an infinite number of attempts per turn. The game master should enforce reasonable limits on how many zero-complexity actions can be attempted per phase.

    Task Complexity
    Moving 1
    Unarmed melee attack 1
    Speaking shortly 0
    Interacting with an environmental object 1
    Dropping into a prone position 0
    Standing up from a prone position 1

    The complexity of actions involving weapons, magic or technology are defined by the setting rules.

    Movement
    Characters can use a movement action to move through the environment during combat.
    Speed Feet Squares Hexes
    Walk 30 6 6
    Run 60 12 12
    Sprint 90 18 18

    This speeds listed in the table can be modified by traits or conditional modifiers. For example, a character carrying a heavy item may move more slowly, while a character who is an athlete may have a trait that allows them to move faster.

    Different movement speeds apply different conditional modifiers to skills used later in the phase at the game master's discretion. For example, sprinting before lifting a heavy object might incur a negative conditional modifier on the lift action, while sprinting before jumping across a gap might grant a positive conditional modifier on the jump action. Repeatedly sprinting might wear a character down, applying a negative conditional modifier on all their actions until they stop to rest.

    Attacking and Defending

    When a character attacks another character (whether it be with melee weapon, ranged weapon or magic-like ability) they use the associated attack skill to make an opposed a skill check against their target. The character initiating the attack is the attacker and the character being attacked is the defender. If the defender is wielding a weapon that has an associated defense skill, they roll an opposed skill check of that skill. Otherwise, the defender rolls two six-sided dice and accepts the base result in place of the skill check.

    If the attacker wins the opposed skill check by 10 or greater, a flawless attack occurs and a special effect may apply. If the defender wins the opposed skill check by 10 or greater, they perform a flawless defense and a special effect may apply. The special effect for a flawless attack or defense is applied before the normal effect of the attack.

    Remember, the Flawless Attack and Flawless Defense Effects occurs in addition to the normal attack effect!

    The resulting effects are based on three factors: Whether the attack skill check succeeds or fails, whether or not a flawless attack or defense occurs, and the type of the attack (melee, ranged, or other).

    Melee

    Melee attacks are performed by characters using melee weapons and can normally only be attempted against characters up to 5 feet away. A melee attacker may choose to move up to 5 feet as part of their attack action.

    Advantage

    Advantage is a relationship between two characters in melee combat where one is in a temporary superior state to the other. In order for an attack to effect the defender's condition, the attacker must have advantage over the defender. Advantage is a one-way relationship; if a character gains advantage over a target, that target loses any advantage they had against the character. A character can have advantage over multiple opponents at once. A character may gain advantage over another in any of the following ways:

  • The character successfully attacked a target and the target has not successfully attacked the character since then.
  • At the start of their phase or action, the game master determines that the character is in a situation that grants advantage over the other (such as flanking the target or being on higher ground).
  • The character performs a flawless defense in melee, immediately granting advantage to the defending character.

    • Advantage is carried over between phases. Advantage resets when the characters move more than 15 feet away from each other. (i.e., neither character has advantage over the other).

    Normal Effect

    The effect of a melee attack is governed by whether the attack succeeds and whether the attacker has advantage over the defender. If the attacker does not already have advantage over the defender, they gain advantage. If the attacker has advantage over the defender, the defender reduces their appropriate condition type by one level.

    Attack Skill Check Attacker Advantage Effect
    Succeed No Attacker gains advantage
    Succeed Yes Defender reduces condition by 1
    Fail No No effect
    Fail Yes No effect
    Flawless Attack Effect

    If the attacker does not already have advantage over the defender, the attacker immediate gains advantage over the defender. If the attacker has advantage over the defender, the defender reduces their appropriate condition type by one levels.

    Flawless Defense Effect

    If the defender does not already have advantage over the attacker, they immediately gain advantage over the attacker. If the defender has advantage over the attacker, they perform a flawless defense but no additional effects occur.

    Ranged

    Ranged attacks are performed by attackers using ranged weapons and can normally be attempted against characters within line of sight and within the weapon's range.

    Ranged attacks are less likely to hit characters in soft cover. If an object partially or fully obscures a defender from an attacker, the defender is said to have soft cover from the attack and an appropriate negative conditional modifier is applied to the attacker.

    Ranged attacks can not hit characters in hard cover. If an object fully obscures a defender from an attacker and the cover is made of a material that the attacker's projectile can not penetrate, the defender is said to have hard cover from the the attack and the attack can never succeed.

    Normal Effect

    If a ranged attack succeeds, the defender reduces their appropriate condition type by one level. If a ranged attack fails, the defender does not reduce their appropriate condition type.

    Flawless Attack Effect

    The attacker performs a flawless attack and the defender reduces their appropriate condition type by one level.

    Flawless Defense Effect

    The defender may choose to move up to five feet in any direction.

    Magic/Tech

    Rules for magical and technological attacks should be implemented by setting rules.

    Prepared Actions

    A character can choose to execute an action during another side's phase by declaring a prepared action. A character must forfeit one of their actions during their own side's phase in order to prepare an action. The character's controlling player must specifically state what action the character will perform. They may then execute that action at any time before their next phase.

    Multitasking and Rushing

    Characters may choose to take more than two actions during a phase by multitasking and/or rushing. Multitasking allows a character to perform two simultaneous actions in place of a single action. Multitasking applies a negative conditional modifier to both actions, equal to one-half the character's skill check for each action. Rushing allows a character to perform three sequential actions during a single phase in place of two sequential actions. Rushing applies a negative conditional modifier to all three actions, equal to one-half the character's skill check for each action. Both multitasking and rushing increase the consequences of failing an action. Multitasking and rushing can be combined, but the negative conditional modifiers should stack appropriately.
